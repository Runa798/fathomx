## 1. Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Competitive Analysis

### What it is
JTBD reframes competitive analysis from feature-to-feature comparison to outcome-to-outcome comparison. The unit of analysis is not the product but the "job" — the functional, emotional, and social progress a customer is trying to make in a specific situation. Competitors are therefore defined as any solution the customer could "hire" to do that job, not just products in the same category.

### Primary source
- Christensen, Clayton M. "Finding the Right Job for Your Product." *MIT Sloan Management Review*, Vol. 48, No. 3, Spring 2007, pp. 38–47. URL: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/finding-the-right-job-for-your-product/
- Christensen, Clayton M. "Integrating Around the Job to Be Done." Available at https://ellisonchair.tamu.edu/files/2020/10/Integrating-Around-the-Job-to-Be-Done.pdf
- Christensen, Clayton M., Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David Duncan. *Competing Against Luck*. Harper Business, 2016. (Book — canonical JTBD competitive application)
- Klement, Alan. *When Coffee and Kale Compete*. 2018. (Foreword co-authored with Ulwick, Moesta, Murtaugh, Wesson as original JTBD co-inventors.) PDF: https://myservicemonitor.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/WCAKC.pdf

### How it concretely works
1. **Write a job statement:** "When [situation], I want to [motivation] so I can [expected outcome]." This anchors every downstream comparison.
2. **Identify the true competitive set:** Includes non-obvious alternatives. In Christensen's milkshake case (HBS): the milkshake's competitors were bananas, bagels, and boredom — not other milkshakes.
3. **Decompose the job into 8–12 chronological steps.** For each step, map three outcome dimensions: functional, emotional, social.
4. **Score competitors per step:** How well does each competitor satisfy each outcome at each step? Gaps reveal under-served and over-served segments.
5. **Map "forces of progress":** Four forces act on switching — push of current situation, pull of new solution, anxiety of the new, habit of the present (Christensen/Moesta).

**Key distinction between two JTBD schools (important for FathomX):**
- **Christensen/Moesta "Switch Interview" school:** Qualitative, narrative, anthropological. Uncovers switching dynamics. Best for finding non-obvious competitors and unmet emotional/social jobs.
- **Ulwick "Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)" school:** Quantitative. Surveys 50–150 desired outcomes, scores importance and satisfaction, applies an Opportunity Algorithm. Best for ranking competitive gaps numerically.

### Validation status
**Practitioner framework** (extensively case-validated) + **academically debated** (core causality model questioned). The Christensen Institute cites widespread application but acknowledges the theory is "widely misunderstood" in practice (Christensen, 2006). MIT Sloan (2015) found empirical testing of JTBD as a causal theory remains limited. Yu-kai Chou (2026) documents three real failure modes: empirically unfalsifiable as stated, conflates description with causation, and the functional/emotional/social hierarchy is a heuristic not a measurement.

**Bottom line:** JTBD is industry-standard and case-validated. Use it for framing the competitive question (what job?) and identifying true competitors. Do not present the outcome scoring as statistically validated science.

### Real practice cases
1. **Christensen's milkshake study (HBS):** Fast food chain discovered morning milkshake customers were "hiring" it for commute companionship and satiety — not refreshment. True competitors: bananas, bagels, boredom. Source: Christensen (2007), MIT Sloan Review, URL above. ✅ Verified.
2. **Cordis Corporation (Johnson & Johnson):** ODI/JTBD applied by Tony Ulwick. Mapped interventional cardiologists' desired outcomes in angioplasty balloon procedures. Market share went from 1% to 20% after 19 new products, all of which became #1 or #2 in their categories. Source: Strategyn.com/tony-ulwick, https://strategyn.com/outcome-driven-innovation/competitive-analysis/ ✅ Retrieved.
3. **Christensen Institute — postsecondary education (2024):** Michael B. Horn and Bob Moesta conducted 200+ switch interviews with students, identifying five distinct JTBD for enrolling in higher education. Source: https://www.christenseninstitute.org/theory/jobs-to-be-done/ ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Define the job-based competitive set (true alternatives, not just category peers)
- **Report section:** "Who are the real competitors?" — opens the competitive report by reframing the analysis unit
- **Opportunity matrix:** Job step × outcome dimension gaps → directly maps to FathomX's "underserved opportunity" cells

## 2. Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) — Opportunity Algorithm

### What it is
ODI is Tony Ulwick's quantitative operationalization of JTBD. Customers are surveyed on 50–150 "desired outcomes" (solution-independent, measurable statements of what they want to achieve). Each outcome is rated on **importance** (1–10) and **current satisfaction** (1–10). The Opportunity Algorithm scores each outcome:

**Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)**

High importance + low satisfaction = highest opportunity. This produces a ranked competitive gap map showing where competitors under-serve customers.

### Primary source
- Ulwick, Tony. "Turn Customer Input into Innovation." *Harvard Business Review*, January 2002. (First public HBR introduction of ODI.) URL: https://hbr.org/2002/01/turn-customer-input-into-innovation *(Note: retrieved via Strategyn page reference — HBR article paywalled but Strategyn page confirms publication.)*
- Ulwick, Tony. *What Customers Want*. McGraw-Hill, 2005. (Book-length treatment.) URL: https://strategyn.com/what-customers-want/
- Strategyn ODI process page: https://strategyn.com/outcome-driven-innovation/competitive-analysis/ ✅ Retrieved.

### How it concretely works
1. Conduct JTBD discovery to define the core job and map all job steps.
2. Generate 50–150 desired outcome statements (solution-independent, measurable: "minimize the time it takes to…", "reduce the likelihood that…").
3. Survey customers: rate importance + satisfaction for each outcome against current solutions (your product and competitors').
4. Apply the Opportunity Algorithm to each outcome. Score >10 = under-served (opportunity). Score <10 with low importance = over-served (cut).
5. Segment customers by outcome priorities (not demographics) to find hidden market segments.
6. Produce an **Opportunity Landscape** map: x-axis = satisfaction, y-axis = importance; quadrants reveal where to invest vs. where to cut.

### Validation status
**Practitioner framework** (1,000+ consulting engagements claimed by Strategyn). Ulwick claims an 86% success rate vs. 17% industry average — these figures come from Strategyn's own reporting and have not been independently peer-reviewed. Treat as practitioner-validated, not academically validated.

### Real practice cases
- **Cordis Corporation:** (Same as above.) ODI identified cardiologists' 50+ desired outcomes for angioplasty. Market share 1% → 20%. Source: Strategyn, URL above. ✅
- **Strategyn: 1,000+ Fortune 100 engagements** (self-reported, not independently verified). Treat as directional.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Quantified competitive gap scoring (importance × satisfaction matrix)
- **Report section:** "Competitive opportunity map" — ranks where competitors fail customers most severely
- **Opportunity matrix:** The Opportunity Algorithm is the most direct input to FathomX's opportunity matrix scoring logic

## 3. Feature Comparison Matrix (Feature Teardown)

### What it is
A structured side-by-side table mapping features/capabilities across competitors. When done well it goes beyond checkbox parity ("has X / doesn't have X") to evidence-based scoring with customer-validated importance weights. The LogRocket PM guide distinguishes superficial teardowns (UI description) from deep teardowns (functionality + usability + technical implementation + business alignment).

### Primary source
- Adeniyi, Kayode. "What is a product teardown? Process, tools, and other insights." *LogRocket Blog*, January 2025. URL: https://blog.logrocket.com/product-management/product-teardown-process-tools/ ✅ Retrieved.
- Institute of Product Management. "How to Do AI Product Teardowns That Build Real PM Skills." URL: https://www.institutepm.com/knowledge-hub/ai-pm-product-teardowns ✅ Retrieved.
- Rezvani, Alireza. "Competitive Teardown — Agent Skill for Product Teams." URL: https://alirezarezvani.github.io/claude-skills/skills/product-team/competitive-teardown/ ✅ Retrieved. (12-dimension scoring rubric with evidence requirements per cell.)

### How it concretely works
**Standard 4-layer teardown structure (InstPM framework):**
1. **UX layer:** Onboarding flow, TTFV (time to first value), friction points, cognitive load. Evidence: screenshots, session recordings, onboarding step counts.
2. **Architecture/capability layer:** What the product actually does vs. what it claims. For AI products: is it RAG, single LLM call, multi-agent? Latency as a signal (2s = single call, 15s = chain).
3. **Data strategy layer:** What data flywheel exists? Does user interaction improve the model? (Spotify Discover Weekly = tight flywheel; generic writing assistant = weak/none.)
4. **Business model layer:** Pricing structure, unit economics, CAC/LTV implications, go-to-market motion.

**12-dimension scoring rubric** (Rezvani, retrieved):
Features, Pricing, UX, Performance, Docs, Support, Integrations, Security, Scalability, Brand, Community, Innovation — each scored 1–5 with evidence per cell required.

**Critical quality gate:** Every cell must have a supporting evidence note (review quote count, specific onboarding step count, feature page screenshot timestamp). A feature matrix without evidence is an opinion list.

### Validation status
**Practitioner framework / industry standard.** No peer-reviewed validation. Universally used by product teams.

### Real practice cases
1. **Facebook vs. MySpace competitive teardown** (Shauchenka, 2025): Documented how Facebook's strategy canvas compared to MySpace — real-names social graph, News Feed as attention engine, platform play, mobile shift. Source: https://www.uladshauchenka.com/p/the-product-managers-teardown-how-facebook-won-the-social-war ✅ Retrieved.
2. **Project management tools teardown** (Barie.ai, 2026): Full competitive teardown of top 5 project management tools (ClickUp, Linear, Monday, Notion, Asana) — features, pricing, positioning, funding, weaknesses. Parallel data collection across 5 dimensions simultaneously to avoid staleness. Source: https://barie.ai/outcome-marketplace/startups-saas/how-barie-runs-a-full-competitive-teardown-of-the-top-5-project-management-tools-features-pricing-positioning-funding-and-weaknesses/ ✅ Retrieved. (Key insight: one PM's 4-day manual teardown was partially wrong before anyone used it — real-time data retrieval is essential.)
3. **GitLab Kano survey for 12 features** (GitLab Handbook, 2026): GitLab product UX team conducted a formal Kano survey to prioritize 12 competing feature candidates. Source: https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/product/ux/experience-research/kano-model/ ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Capability parity matrix (what exists where, scored with evidence)
- **Report section:** "Feature landscape" — the factual backbone of any competitive report
- **Opportunity matrix:** Over-served features (everyone has them at high quality) = commoditized; under-served = gaps

## 4. Kano Model Applied to Competitive Feature Comparison

### What it is
The Kano Model categorizes product features by their effect on customer satisfaction into five types: Must-be (Basic), Performance (One-dimensional), Attractive (Delighters), Indifferent, and Reverse. Applied competitively: Must-be features define table stakes (if you lack them, customers won't choose you). Performance features are where competitive scores differentiate. Attractive features are where you can win decisively if you offer them while competitors don't.

### Primary source
- **Original paper:** Kano, Noriaki; Nobuhiku Seraku; Fumio Takahashi; Shinichi Tsuji. "Attractive quality and must-be quality." *Journal of the Japanese Society for Quality Control*, Vol. 14, No. 2, April 1984, pp. 147–156. ISSN 0386-8230. URL: https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/quality/14/2/14_KJ00002952366/_article/-char/en ✅ Retrieved (abstract confirmed, full article in Japanese behind paywall).
- **Academic review:** Witell, Lars, Martin Löfgren, and Jens Jörn Dahlgaard. "Theory of attractive quality and the Kano methodology — the past, the present, and the future." *Total Quality Management and Business Excellence*, Vol. 24, Nos. 11–12, 2013, pp. 1241–1252. URL: https://liu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:666390/FULLTEXT02.pdf ✅ Retrieved. (Reviews 147 research papers, 1984–2012; confirms theory is academically well-validated.)

### How it concretely works
**Competitive application:**
1. List all features across your product and key competitors.
2. Run a Kano questionnaire: for each feature, ask both functional ("How do you feel if this feature IS present?") and dysfunctional ("How do you feel if this feature is NOT present?") questions.
3. Cross-reference responses to classify each feature per competitor: Must-be / Performance / Attractive / Indifferent / Reverse.
4. **Competitive read:** Features that are Must-be for your category but missing from a competitor = that competitor's table-stakes gap. Features that are Attractive for your product but Indifferent for a competitor = your potential differentiator.
5. Kano also reveals feature migration over time: Attractive features become Performance, Performance becomes Must-be. This maps disruption trajectories.

**Prodify group recommendation:** Use Kano competitively by reviewing all competitors' product pages to identify Must-be baseline (what every product has in common), then qualitative interviews to validate.

### Validation status
**Academically validated** (peer-reviewed, 147 papers 1984–2012, Witell et al. 2013 review). Built on Herzberg's motivation-hygiene theory. One of the most rigorously tested product frameworks.

### Real practice cases
1. **GitLab — 12-feature Kano survey** (GitLab Handbook, 2026): UX research team ran formal Kano survey for 12 new features before building. Produced clear Must-be / Performance / Attractive classification for roadmap prioritization. Source: https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/product/ux/experience-research/kano-model/ ✅ Retrieved.
2. **Indian fintech (productgrowth.in):** Used Kano to distinguish UPI PIN requirement (Must-be / negative when present = Reverse) vs. cashback rates (Performance / linear). Led to UX redesign removing 6-digit PIN for sub-₹500 transactions. Source: https://productgrowth.in/resources/frameworks/kano-model/ ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Feature classification layer — separates table stakes from differentiators from irrelevant features
- **Report section:** "What actually matters to users?" — Kano overlay on the feature matrix
- **Opportunity matrix:** Attractive features nobody else has = high-priority opportunity cells

## 5. Perceptual / Positioning Map (Strategy Canvas)

### What it is
A two-axis visual tool that plots your product and competitors on dimensions that drive real buyer decisions. The **perceptual map** (customer-perception-based, Avery/HBS framing) and the **strategy canvas** (Blue Ocean Strategy tool, Kim & Mauborgne) are related but distinct:
- Perceptual map: plots brands as customers perceive them. Axes from customer research (factor analysis, MDS).
- Strategy canvas: plots relative investment/offering levels across competitive factors. Axes from competitive landscape, not customer perception alone.

### Primary source
- **Blue Ocean Strategy:** Kim, W. Chan, and Renée Mauborgne. "Blue Ocean Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, October 2004. URL: https://hbr.org/2004/10/blue-ocean-strategy ✅ Confirmed (HBR paywall, article confirmed to exist).
- **Strategy Canvas tool page:** https://www.blueoceanstrategy.com/tools/strategy-canvas/ ✅ Retrieved.
- **Strategy canvas examples (iPhone, Comic Relief, Cirque du Soleil):** https://www.blueoceanstrategy.com/blog/strategy-canvas-examples/ ✅ Retrieved.
- **HBS perceptual map:** Avery, Jill. "Creating Brand Value" (HBS course material). Described in: https://online.hbs.edu/blog/post/perceptual-map ✅ Retrieved.

### How it concretely works
**Strategy Canvas (Kim & Mauborgne):**
1. List the key factors the industry competes on (horizontal axis).
2. For each factor, score your product and each competitor's offering level (vertical axis, 1–5 or high/medium/low).
3. Connect the dots to create a "value curve" per competitor.
4. Apply the **Four Actions Framework**: Eliminate (factors that don't matter to buyers), Reduce (over-invested factors), Raise (under-invested factors), Create (new factors nobody offers).
5. White space = factors with low competition and rising buyer demand = blue ocean opportunity.

**Critical execution requirement:** Axes must reflect real buyer decision drivers, not internal assumptions. Test: "If you can plot every competitor without looking anything up, your axes are too generic." (themarketingjuice.com, retrieved.)

**Real case — iPhone strategy canvas (Blue Ocean site):**
Apple's 2007 iPhone value curve diverged sharply from Nokia/Motorola on factors like ease-of-use, internet experience, and ecosystem — while reducing traditional carrier-relationship and keyboard factors. This visual made the strategic choice legible.

### Validation status
**Practitioner framework** (widely used). Blue Ocean Strategy is cited >10,000 times academically (Kim & Mauborgne are INSEAD professors). The strategy canvas tool itself has not been independently validated as causally producing better outcomes. Use for communication and hypothesis generation, not as a proof mechanism.

### Real practice cases
1. **iPhone vs. incumbents (2007):** Blue Ocean strategy canvas documented how Apple's value curve diverged from Nokia/Motorola on internet experience and ease-of-use while reducing form-factor complexity. Source: https://www.blueoceanstrategy.com/blog/strategy-canvas-examples/ ✅ Retrieved.
2. **Comic Relief UK charity:** Strategy canvas showed all major UK charities had near-identical strategic profiles. Comic Relief diverged by creating entertainment-based fundraising (e.g., Red Nose Day TV events), eliminating traditional "pity plea" mechanics. Source: https://www.blueoceanstrategy.com/blog/visualize-strategy-stand-apart-case-comic-relief/ ✅ Retrieved.
3. **B2B SaaS (Reforge artifact — Claire, Blue Ocean repositioning):** Consultant used Blue Ocean strategy canvas to show client's positioning was wrong (targeting low-cost SMBs when their real strength was high-cost custom dev). Presented to Executive Committee to justify new positioning investment. Source: https://www.reforge.com/artifacts/value-proposition-and-competitor-comparison ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Positioning layer — where does each competitor sit relative to buyer decision axes?
- **Report section:** "Competitive positioning map" — a visual artifact included in every FathomX competitive report
- **Opportunity matrix:** White-space quadrants = blue ocean opportunity inputs

## 6. Christensen Disruption Theory — Competitive Threat Classification

### What it is
Christensen's disruption theory distinguishes two competitive threat types:
- **Sustaining innovation:** Improves existing products along the dimensions incumbents already compete on. Incumbents almost always win these battles.
- **Disruptive innovation:** Starts at the low end or in new markets with "good enough" products. Initially ignored by incumbents because margins are low. Over time, performance improves and disrupts the mainstream market.

For competitive analysis, this framework answers: "Is this competitor a sustaining threat (compete head-on) or a disruptive threat (ignore at your peril)?"

### Primary source
- Christensen, Clayton M. *The Innovator's Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail*. Harvard Business School Press, 1997. HBS record: https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=46 ✅ Confirmed.
- McDonald, Rory, and Clayton M. Christensen. "Disruptive Innovation: An Intellectual History and Directions for Future Research." *Journal of Management Studies*, 2018. DOI: 10.1111/joms.12349. URL: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joms.12349 ✅ Retrieved (abstract confirmed). Key finding: "core concepts remain widely misunderstood."
- **MIT Sloan critical assessment:** "How Useful Is the Theory of Disruptive Innovation?" *MIT Sloan Management Review*, September 2015. URL: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/how-useful-is-the-theory-of-disruptive-innovation/ ✅ Retrieved.

### How it concretely works
**Competitive threat classification checklist:**
1. Where does the competitor enter the market? Low end (low-margin, underserved) or new market (non-consumers)?
2. Is their current product "good enough" for a specific segment that incumbents over-serve?
3. Is their performance trajectory improving along dimensions that will eventually satisfy mainstream customers?
4. Does their business model make it structurally difficult for incumbents to respond (e.g., different cost structure, different channel, different customer)?

If yes to all four: classify as **disruptive threat** — requires asymmetric response (separate business unit, different metrics).  
If no: classify as **sustaining threat** — compete directly on your existing performance dimensions.

**Common misuse (McDonald & Christensen, 2018):** Practitioners use "disruption" to describe any new competitor or startup. This overuse causes strategic misdiagnosis — companies either panic unnecessarily at sustaining threats or ignore genuine disruptive threats by mislabeling them as fringe.

### Validation status
**Academically validated with caveats.** Theory originated in peer-reviewed work (1997, HBS Press). McDonald & Christensen (2018, Journal of Management Studies) reviewed the intellectual history and confirmed the theory has evolved significantly — the 2018 version is more causally specified than the 1997 version. MIT Sloan (2015) found empirical testing remains limited. Widely cited (>10,000 academic citations) but empirical falsifiability remains debated.

### Real practice cases
1. **Netflix vs. Blockbuster:** Classic disruption case — Netflix entered with mail-order DVDs (underserved late-adopters, no late fees), improved over time, eventually made Blockbuster's business model structurally unviable. Gibson Biddle (Netflix VP Product) documented how Netflix's "no late fees" positioning was a **counter-positioning** move that Blockbuster could not copy without destroying their core profit source. Source: Biddle, Gibson. "The DHM Model." https://gibsonbiddle.medium.com/2-the-dhm-model-6ea5dfd80792 ✅ Retrieved.
2. **Loom vs. Zoom/Slack:** Loom entered with async video (new market: remote async communication), not competing head-on with Zoom (real-time sync). Repositioned to B2B teams in 2020 during COVID, reached 25M users, acquired by Atlassian for $975M (2023). Source: https://www.productgrowth.blog/p/loom-growth-teardown-975m-dollar-exit and https://sachcode.substack.com/p/the-positioning-pivot-that-saved ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Threat classification layer — sustaining vs. disruptive; trajectory analysis
- **Report section:** "Competitive threat assessment" — separates the threat type per competitor
- **Opportunity matrix:** Disruptive opportunity = low-end or non-consumption gap that incumbents ignore

## 7. Porter's Five Forces — Correct Use & Common Misuse

### What it is
Porter's Five Forces analyzes the structural attractiveness and competitive intensity of an **entire industry** (not a product). The five forces: (1) threat of new entrants, (2) bargaining power of suppliers, (3) bargaining power of buyers, (4) threat of substitutes, (5) rivalry among existing competitors. Together, these determine long-run average industry profitability.

### Primary source
- Porter, Michael E. "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, January 2008. URL: https://hbr.org/2008/01/the-five-competitive-forces-that-shape-strategy ✅ Confirmed (content retrieved, paywalled full text).
- Key quote (retrieved): Competition for profits "goes beyond established industry rivals to include four other competitive forces." "The five forces collectively define an industry's structure and shape the nature of competitive interaction within an industry."

### How it concretely works (correct use only)
1. Define the **industry** precisely (not the company, not the product).
2. Assess each force using multi-year industry-wide data.
3. Use results to decide: Is this industry structurally attractive? Should we enter/exit/invest?
4. Combine with VRIN (value, rarity, inimitability, non-substitutability) analysis for firm-level strategy.

### Validation status
**Academically validated** (Porter is a Harvard professor; framework is peer-reviewed and cited extensively in strategic management literature). Grant, R.M., *Contemporary Strategy Analysis*, 11th ed., Wiley, 2022 — confirms correct scope is industry-level.

### Common misuse (critical for FathomX to avoid)
1. **Product-level application:** Mapping forces onto a single product. Violates the industry-scope requirement. Features and pricing sit *inside* one force (rivalry/substitutes), not across the whole structure.
2. **Static snapshot:** Running it once. Credible applications track shifts across 3–5 years.
3. **Firm-centric metrics:** Using one company's market share instead of industry averages.
4. **Missing complementors:** Modern digital markets require extending with ecosystem/platform dynamics, which classic Five Forces omits.

**Verdict for FathomX:** Five Forces is useful for **market context framing** (e.g., "Is this industry structurally hard to enter? High supplier power?") but is **not a product competitive analysis tool**. Misapplying it at the product level produces misleading signals.

### Real practice cases
**No named product-level teardown case found** — because correct use of Five Forces is at the industry level, not the product level. References to Five Forces in product competitive teardowns are almost always misuse.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Market structure context only — input to the "why does this market exist / what makes it defensible?" section
- **Report section:** "Industry structure" — optional context framing, not a core competitive analysis section
- **Opportunity matrix:** NOT a direct input. Use only to assess market attractiveness of the space being analyzed.

## 8. SWOT Analysis — Correct Use & Documented Limitations

### What it is
SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) maps internal (S/W) and external (O/T) factors into a 2×2 framework. Widely used as a competitive summary tool. Also widely criticized for producing vague, unactionable outputs.

### Primary source
- Hill, Terry, and Roy Westbrook. "SWOT Analysis: It's Time for a Product Recall." *Long Range Planning*, Vol. 30, No. 1, February 1997, pp. 46–52. (Academic critique — first major published critique of SWOT misuse.) Note: citation verified through academic review literature; direct URL not retrieved but journal confirmed.
- Kotler, Philip, and Kevin Lane Keller. *Marketing Management*, 16th ed. Pearson. (Standard reference confirming subjectivity limitation.)

### How it concretely works (correct use)
**SWOT is a synthesis tool, not a discovery tool.** The correct sequence:
1. First conduct rigorous competitive research using JTBD analysis, feature teardown, ODI, positioning maps.
2. Then use SWOT to *summarize and communicate* the findings to non-specialist stakeholders.
3. Convert each SWOT cell into a specific strategic implication (SO/WO/ST/WT strategies).

### Validation status
**Opinion/heuristic.** No peer-reviewed empirical validation that SWOT produces better strategic outcomes. Hill & Westbrook (1997) found in a study of 50 UK companies that SWOT outputs were rarely converted into actionable strategy. Academic consensus: SWOT is a communication tool, not an analysis tool.

### Documented limitations
1. No prioritization mechanism — all factors treated equally.
2. Static: does not model change over time.
3. Subjectivity: factors depend on individual bias.
4. No causal links between quadrants.
5. Generates lists, not decisions.

### Real practice cases
**Reforge artifact — Connected (Sachin Rekhi, 2023):** Competitive landscape analysis for Connected (personal relationship manager). Rekhi explicitly oriented the competitive analysis around **big categories of risk in the product strategy** (business model risk, growth risk) rather than a generic SWOT. This risk-first framing identified business model risks (no competitor had successfully monetized similar products) that shaped the entire next validation phase. Source: https://www.reforge.com/artifacts/new-product-landscape-analysis-connected ✅ Retrieved.

**Lesson:** Rekhi's approach is what SWOT should be replaced with — risk-first competitive analysis that identifies kill conditions for the strategy, not a generic 2×2.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** Use only as the final summary layer after all evidence is collected
- **Report section:** "Strategic summary" — synthesizes findings for executive/stakeholder communication
- **Opportunity matrix:** NOT a primary input. Convert SWOT outputs into specific opportunity/threat entries with evidence.

## 9. Marty Cagan / SVPG — Competitor Analysis Practice

### What it is
Marty Cagan (Silicon Valley Product Group) offers a practitioner-level prescription for how product leaders should study competitors. His approach is notably minimalist compared to McKinsey-style frameworks — it prioritizes customer value delivery over competitive response.

### Primary source
- Cagan, Marty. "Competition." SVPG, August 2, 2011. URL: https://www.svpg.com/competition/ ✅ Confirmed (content retrieved, direct URL returned 403 — but Exa retrieved full content).

### How it concretely works
Cagan's concrete prescription (from retrieved content):
1. **Study each competitor to identify the 3 things they do that provide real value for their customers** (strengths — functional, UX, policy, pricing, service, anything).
2. **Identify the 3 major areas where the competitor fails to meet customer needs** (weaknesses — same dimensions).
3. **Take a holistic view:** Strengths and weaknesses may be in functionality, policies, customer service, pricing, or anything else.
4. **Avoid both extremes:** Neither obsess over competitors (stress) nor ignore them (miss learning opportunities).
5. **Competitor-proof through customer value, not feature response:** "Stop chasing your competitors and start focusing on providing real value for your customers."

**Cagan's assessment framework ("Product Opportunity Assessment", 2006, SVPG):**
10 questions including: "What alternatives are out there?" (competitive landscape), "Why are we best suited to pursue this?" (differentiator), "Why now?" (market window). Source: https://www.svpg.com/assessing-product-opportunities/ ✅ Retrieved.

### Validation status
**Opinion/heuristic** (practitioner-authority framework from an influential PM community leader). Not peer-reviewed.

### Real practice cases
**Netflix DHM Model (Gibson Biddle, former VP Product):** Netflix applied an analogous "3 strengths / 3 weaknesses per competitor" logic through consumer science (quantitative research, A/B testing, qualitative). Biddle documented that Netflix's "no late fees" was a counter-positioning move that studied Blockbuster's weaknesses (late fees = primary profit source) and exploited the structural impossibility of Blockbuster copying them. Source: https://gibsonbiddle.medium.com/2-the-dhm-model-6ea5dfd80792 ✅ Retrieved.

### FathomX landing point
- **Competitive dimension:** "Top 3 strengths / top 3 weaknesses" per competitor — a fast, non-exhaustive starting point for each competitor profile
- **Report section:** "Competitor profiles" — each profile starts with Cagan's 3+3 format before drilling deeper
- **Opportunity matrix:** Weakness clusters across multiple competitors = shared market failure = opportunity cell

## Synthesis: MECE Competitive Research Dimensions for FathomX

Based on all retrieved evidence, a MECE competitive report structure for FathomX should cover exactly 5 dimensions (in order):

| # | Dimension | Primary Tool | Evidence Standard |
|---|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Job & True Competitors** | JTBD (Christensen/Moesta switch interview) | Named job statement + non-obvious alternatives identified |
| 2 | **Capability Parity Matrix** | Feature teardown (12-dimension rubric) | Each cell scored 1–5 with evidence citation |
| 3 | **Feature Importance Classification** | Kano model | Must-be / Performance / Attractive per feature per competitor |
| 4 | **Competitive Gap Scoring** | ODI Opportunity Algorithm | Importance × Satisfaction scoring → ranked opportunity list |
| 5 | **Positioning & White Space** | Strategy Canvas / Perceptual Map | Buyer-validated axes, plotted value curves, white-space identification |

**Supporting layers (not MECE-core, but standard report sections):**
- Threat classification (Christensen disruption theory) → "Sustaining vs. disruptive threat" per competitor
- Market structure context (Porter Five Forces) → optional, industry-level only
- Strategic summary (SWOT) → communication layer only, after evidence is complete
- Competitor profiles (Cagan 3+3) → fast-format narrative summary per competitor
