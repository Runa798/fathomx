# B3: Product-Expert Thinking — Sources, Frameworks, Encodable Directives

**Research question:** What makes product analysis have genuine "product-expert flavor" rather than generic/superficial commentary? Operationalize the thinking patterns of senior product experts into concrete, encodable directives.

**Scope:** Primary sources from Marty Cagan (SVPG), Shreyas Doshi, Julie Zhuo, Lenny Rachitsky, Ben Horowitz, and supporting thinkers. All sources verified by direct URL retrieval unless otherwise noted.

---

## 1. Marty Cagan (SVPG)

### Core Thesis on Product Sense

Cagan argues that what people call "product sense" is better described as **deep product knowledge** — built through sustained immersion rather than innate talent.

> "I argue that strong product sense is better described as deep product knowledge, and is the result of truly immersing yourself into a specific product space. You build strong product sense by spending serious quality time with customers, analyzing the competitive landscape, immersing yourself in the product data, and consuming everything you can about the industry, and the enabling technologies."

Source: Marty Cagan, "Product Sense Demystified," SVPG, 2022-10-04. URL: https://www.svpg.com/product-sense-demystified/ (confirmed via Exa search; direct fetch returned 403, but Exa retrieved verbatim text)

### The Four Big Risks Framework

Every product idea must be validated against four systemic risks before building:

1. **Value risk** — Will customers buy or use this?
2. **Usability risk** — Can users figure out how to use it?
3. **Feasibility risk** — Can we build it with current technology and skills?
4. **Viability risk** — Does this work for the business (economics, legal, compliance)?

Source: Marty Cagan, *Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love*, 2nd ed. (2017), Chapters 4–6; also SVPG article "The Four Big Risks." URL: https://www.svpg.com (book is primary; confirmed via Exa search).

### Four Critical Areas of PM Contribution

Strong PMs must master:

1. **Deep knowledge of users and customers** — "The product manager needs to be the acknowledged expert on the customer. Without this deep customer knowledge, the product manager is just guessing."
2. **Deep knowledge of the data** — both quantitative (what users do) and qualitative (why they do it)
3. **Deep knowledge of the business** — go-to-market, business model, economics, stakeholder needs
4. **Deep knowledge of the industry** — competitors, tech trends, customer behavior shifts; "we must create products for where the market will be tomorrow, not where it used to be yesterday"

Source: Marty Cagan, "The Product Manager Contribution," SVPG, 2012-03-18. URL: https://www.svpg.com/the-product-manager-contribution/ (Exa-retrieved verbatim text)

### Outcome Ownership vs. Output Execution

Strong PMs own outcomes (business results and customer impact). Weak PMs own features or roadmaps and act as order-takers. This is the SVPG distinction between empowered product teams and feature teams.

> "Empowered teams don't need less leadership; they need better leadership."

Source: Marty Cagan, "Empowered Product Teams," SVPG, 2018-10-31. URL: https://www.svpg.com/empowered-product-teams/ (Exa-retrieved)

### Customer-Centricity as Practice

Cagan describes coaching PMs to visit 30 customers (15 US, 15 Europe) before making a single product decision. The goal is establishing the reputation of being "someone that has a deep and personal knowledge of the company's users and customers."

Source: Marty Cagan, "Coaching – Customer-Centricity," SVPG, 2019-10-24. URL: https://www.svpg.com/coaching-customer-centricity/ (Exa-retrieved)

### Good Product People

> "I define a strong product person as someone that is obsessed with solving tough problems in ways that are loved by their users and customers, yet can sustain a business, and are just now possible."

Source: Marty Cagan, "Good Product People," SVPG, 2024-05-21. URL: https://www.svpg.com/good-product-people/ (Exa-retrieved)

## 2. Shreyas Doshi (ex-Stripe, Twitter, Google, Yahoo)

### Product Sense: Core Definition

> "Product sense is the ability to make correct product decisions, both macro and micro, even in the face of major ambiguity."

Three components:
1. **Empathy** — "the ability to simulate the mental processes of multiple personas that are nothing like you"
2. **Domain knowledge** — knowledge about customers, competition, and technology constraints/opportunities
3. **Creativity** — "the ability to conceive solutions and approaches to solve your product problems in optimal ways"

Source: Shreyas Doshi, interview/course description at McKelvey School of Engineering, Washington University; also reproduced at Exponent, "Developing Your Product Sense," 2023-01-17. URL: https://www.tryexponent.com/blog/product-sense

### Product Sense in the AI Age: Five-Component Decomposition

In a more recent (2026) formulation, Doshi expands product sense to five interconnected capabilities:

1. **Strong empathy** — discovering unmet needs beyond AI analysis
2. **Simulation skills** — identifying future possibilities through domain understanding
3. **Strategic thinking** — determining key segments and competitive differentiation
4. **Great taste** — selecting optimal recommendations aligned with business goals
5. **Creative execution** — conceiving unique solutions competitors cannot replicate

> "The only real long-term career moat for product people is how you can improve on the already-brilliant, already-comprehensive inputs and outputs that AI will provide for you."

Source: Shreyas Doshi, "Why Product Sense is the only product skill that will matter in the AI age," Substack, 2026-03-05. URL: https://shreyasdoshi.substack.com/p/why-product-sense-is-the-only-product (direct fetch confirmed)

### Good PMs vs. Great PMs Thread (selected points most relevant to thinking patterns)

> "2/ Good PMs make metrics-driven product decisions. Great PMs make metrics-informed product decisions. Great PMs are masters of the art of blending quantitative and qualitative inputs, as warranted by each individual situation."

> "5/ Good PMs make product decisions based on a thoroughly logical and rational view of the world. Great PMs view logic and reason as important tools, but they also know that people—and therefore the users & customers of their product—are driven by emotion more than by logic."

> "6/ Good PMs know the importance of talking to customers frequently. Good PMs listen intently to customers to develop their product hypothesis. Great PMs also listen to what isn't said and anticipate where the industry overall is headed when developing their product hypothesis."

> "8/ Good PMs seek to validate their ideas & instincts with trusted colleagues. They often ask: 'Does this make sense?' Great PMs make it as easy as possible for colleagues to push back on their ideas & instincts. They often ask: 'What am I getting wrong?'"

> "Good PMs ensure that the product strategy itself is optimal [before focusing on execution]."

Source: Shreyas Doshi, Twitter/X thread, 2020-04-11. Thread ID 1249039638829793280. URL: https://threadreaderapp.com/thread/1249039638829793280.html (direct fetch confirmed)

### LNO Framework (Leverage / Neutral / Overhead)

Published February 2, 2020 on Twitter (thread), later formalized at coda.io/@shreyas/lno-framework.

- **Leverage (L)** — work where effort yields 10x–100x return
- **Neutral (N)** — work where 1 unit of effort yields ~1.1x return
- **Overhead (O)** — necessary work that yields less than 1x return

> "All your tasks are not created equal. Doing great work doesn't mean that you put in your best effort for every task."

The insight for product analysis: the same asymmetry applies to claims in a research document — some observations unlock 10x insight; others are Overhead that sounds analytical but returns nothing.

Source: Shreyas Doshi, Twitter thread, 2020-02-02; formalized at https://coda.io/@shreyas/lno-framework (Exa-retrieved verbatim text)

### Three Levels of Product Work (Impact / Execution / Optics)

Three default modes in which product professionals operate:

- **Impact** — thinking about customer outcomes and business results
- **Execution** — thinking about milestones, delivery, speed
- **Optics** — thinking about awareness, excitement, optics for leadership

> "The level at which you think by default matters a lot."

Critical corollary:
> "Most Execution problems are really Strategy problems, Interpersonal problems, or Culture problems. Leaders who recognize this address root causes; those who don't stick Execution band-aids on very deep wounds."

Source: Shreyas Doshi, podcast with Lenny Rachitsky, 2022. Transcript summary at https://podcastsdistilled.com/lennys-podcast-product-career-growth/shreyas-doshi-on-pre-mortems-the-lno-framework-the-three-levels-of-product-work-why-most-execution/ (direct fetch confirmed)

### Pre-Mortem Technique

A ritual where a team imagines the project has *already failed* and works backward to identify causes. Generates three categories:

- **Tigers** — actual threats
- **Paper Tigers** — perceived but unlikely threats
- **Elephants** — unspoken concerns no one is voicing

> "Why do we need to wait until after things go wrong? Why can't we extract these insights before they go wrong?"

Source: Same Lenny Rachitsky podcast, 2022 (confirmed above)

### Experiment Discipline

> "When you are running an experiment, you need to specify your objectives, success/failure criteria, and decision tree upfront. Without these things, it becomes a Validation, not an Experiment."

> "If you have just shipped a product, but don't yet have a way of getting usage data for the product, you have not really shipped the product. You have just shipped functionality."

Source: Shreyas Doshi, Twitter thread, 2021-01-26. Thread ID 1352860350719303681. URL: https://threadreaderapp.com/thread/1352860350719303681.html (direct fetch confirmed)

### Confirmation Bias in Customer Research

> "If you are talking to customers with a certain product idea already in your mind, you will usually manage to find great reasons why it makes sense to build that idea. Starting with a blank slate keeps a product manager's biggest enemy—confirmation bias—at bay."

Source: Same thread 1352860350719303681 (confirmed)

### 7 Biases of Product Teams

The framework maps biases to countermeasures:

| Bias | Countermeasure |
|---|---|
| Focusing Illusion (tunnel on one problem) | Deep Customer Empathy |
| IKEA Effect (over-value what you built) | First Principles Reasoning |
| Bias-for-Building (build before specifying) | Velocity (speed + direction) |
| Execution Orientation (optimize for what's easy now) | High Ambition and Agency |
| Maslow's Hammer (apply one tool everywhere) | Context-First Approach |
| Russian Roulette (ignore catastrophic scenarios) | Catastrophe Prevention Focus |
| Authority Approval Bias (optimize for executive approval) | Constructive Dissonance Culture |

Source: Shreyas Doshi, Twitter thread, 2020-09-26. Thread ID 1309708343963865088. URL: https://threadreaderapp.com/thread/1309708343963865088 (direct fetch confirmed)

### 5Qs for Product Rigor

A framework for structuring any product proposal or analysis:

1. Who is the customer?
2. What is the problem/opportunity?
3. What is the main customer benefit?
4. How do you know customers want this?
5. What is the customer experience?

Source: Shreyas Doshi, Twitter thread of PM frameworks, 2021-05-31. Thread ID 1399042778613485578. URL: https://threadreaderapp.com/thread/1399042778613485578.html (Exa-retrieved)

## 3. Julie Zhuo (ex-VP Product Design, Facebook; founder Sundial)

### Product Thinking: Core Definition

> "Product thinking [is] the skill of knowing what makes a product useful — and loved — by people."

Zhuo distinguishes:
- **Product thinking** — holistic, intuitive judgment about people-product relationships (the *what* and *why*)
- **Design thinking** — a methodology for iterative problem-solving (the *how*)
- **Product vision** — an idealized end state (the *destination*)

Product thinkers "seek to understand the broader question of why a product might or might not work for a broader set of people." They are "voracious about understanding why things work."

Two core habits for developing product thinking:
1. **Observation** — noticing what delights or frustrates; studying reviews and market reactions
2. **Inquiry** — asking "why" behind observations through behavioral research, cultural analysis, data

Source: Julie Zhuo, "The Power of Product Thinking," Future (a16z), 2021-06-15. URL: https://future.com/product-thinking/ (direct fetch confirmed)

### The Judgement Equation

> "Judgement is the ability to transmute information into actions that achieve the desired outcome."

> **Judgement = Quality of Decision Model + Richness of Data + Clarity of Mind**

- **Quality of decision model** — the subconscious framework built through accumulated pattern recognition; a master engineer's "thousands of subtle signals" processed intuitively
- **Richness of data** — sufficient relevant information; hiring decisions improve dramatically after 3 months vs. 3 hours of observation
- **Clarity of mind** — mental state (fear, anger, stress) degrades all other components regardless of model quality

Source: Julie Zhuo, "The Looking Glass: Sharpening Judgement," Medium, 2024-09-12. URL: https://joulee.medium.com/the-looking-glass-sharpening-judgement-3048373314b0 (direct fetch confirmed)

### Problem-First Principle

> "1. A product succeeds because it solves a problem for people."
> "2. The first step in building something new is understanding what problem you want to solve, and for whom. This should be crystal clear before you start thinking about any solutions."
> "3. The second question you should ask yourself is 'why is this particular problem worth solving.'"

Source: Julie Zhuo, "Building Products," Medium (The Year of the Looking Glass), 2016-06-02. URL: https://medium.com/the-year-of-the-looking-glass/building-products-91aa93bea4bb (Exa-retrieved)

### Hypothesis-Driven Product Development (0→1 Stage)

> "The key in this phase is to define good hypotheses and get to conclusive results on those hypotheses as quickly as possible."

At the 0→1 stage, MVPs must deliver a viable end-to-end experience for a narrowly defined audience — broad audiences require research and data rather than intuition.

Source: Julie Zhuo, "The 4 Stages of 0→1 Products," Medium, 2017-11-25. URL: https://medium.com/the-year-of-the-looking-glass/the-4-stages-of-0-1-products-cdb8236dbf66 (Exa-retrieved)

### Quality and Trade-off Framing

> "When you get into a [quality vs. time] state, the real question to ask is: 'If we knew X would take this long to get to high-quality, would we have opted to do it in the first place?'"

This reframes sunk-cost thinking into prospective trade-off evaluation: if the answer is "no," cut the feature; if "yes," commit fully.

Source: Julie Zhuo, "How to Make Things High-Quality," Medium, 2019-01-09. URL: https://medium.com/the-year-of-the-looking-glass/how-to-make-things-high-quality-f466f875227d (Exa-retrieved)

## 4. Lenny Rachitsky (Lenny's Newsletter)

### Product Sense as Trainable Muscle

Rachitsky's synthesis (2021–2025) consistently frames product sense as learnable through deliberate practice, not innate gift:

Key training practices:
1. **Deep user empathy** — understanding underlying needs, not surface requests
2. **Observe real user behavior** — watching actual users, not imagining usage
3. **Reverse-engineer products** — systematically dissect design decisions to uncover original user problem + business constraint tradeoffs
4. **Study how top product thinkers decide** — pattern-match across documented decisions
5. **Cultivate technology curiosity** — anticipate where market/tech will be, not where it is
6. **Measure your own improvement** — track prediction accuracy over time

Source: Lenny Rachitsky, "How to develop product sense," Lenny's Newsletter, 2021 (audio edition at https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-to-develop-product-sense, published 2025-09-16; references original 2021 post)

### Product Strategy Hierarchy

From Rachitsky's synthesis of top PM frameworks:

> Mission → Vision → Strategy → Goals → Roadmap → Task

This hierarchy is consistently cited in his "best of" lists as foundational reading for building better products.

Source: Lenny Rachitsky, "The Best of Lenny's Newsletter — 2024 Edition," 2024-12-17. URL: https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/the-best-of-lennys-newsletter2024 (Exa-retrieved)

### On What AI Won't Replace

> "I believe that AI will have the most profound impact on the high-level (and historically most valued) skills of product management: developing a strategy, crafting a vision, identifying new opportunities, and setting goals. Furthermore, soft skills like product sense, communication, creativity, and being the glue that enables a team to operate at their very best will become even more important."

> "People stuff! Aligning opinionated stakeholders, unblocking blockers, pushing teams to work harder, creating amazing experiences, getting buy-in on big ideas, understanding and acting on nuance..."

Source: Lenny Rachitsky, "How AI will impact product management," Lenny's Newsletter, 2024-04-09. URL: https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-ai-will-impact-product-management (Exa-retrieved)

## 5. Ben Horowitz (a16z) — Foundational Essay

### Good PM / Bad PM: The Thinking Contrasts

> "Good product managers know the market, the product, the product line and the competition extremely well and operate from a strong basis of knowledge and confidence."

> "Good product managers decompose problems. Bad product managers combine all problems into one."

> "Good PMs think in terms of delivering superior value to the marketplace during inbound planning and achieving market share and revenue goals during outbound. Bad PMs get very confused about the differences amongst delivering value, matching competitive features, pricing, and ubiquity."

> "Good product managers take written positions on important issues (architectural choices, market strategy)."

> "Good product managers probe deeper into the underlying problems to get at the compelling value proposition for the customer. If you had a noisy car you might ask for a louder stereo, but you would probably be a lot happier with a quieter car."

Source: Ben Horowitz, "Good Product Manager/Bad Product Manager," a16z, 2012-06-15 (originally written 1998 with David Weiden). URL: https://a16z.com/good-product-manager-bad-product-manager/ (direct fetch confirmed)

## 6. Supporting Frameworks and Thinkers

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) — Christensen / Ulwick

The canonical framework for grounding product analysis in user jobs rather than features:

- **Job statement** structure: "When [situation], I want to [motivation] so I can [expected outcome]"
- **Feature path**: sequence of capabilities that progressively reduce friction across the job's timeline
- **Experience gap**: measurable difference between current satisfaction and desired outcome for each job step; large gaps = innovation opportunities

Source: Clayton Christensen, *The Innovator's Solution* (2003); Tony Ulwick, *What Customers Want* (2005) and *Competing Against Luck* (Christensen et al., 2016). (Practitioner/opinion framework — widely cited but not empirically validated as a universal law)

### Strong Opinions, Weakly Held — Paul Saffo

A decision-making framework for operating under uncertainty:

- Form a strong hypothesis (act with conviction)
- Actively seek *disconfirming* evidence
- Change the hypothesis when disconfirmation arrives

> "You want to actively prove yourself wrong by seeking disconfirming evidence... if you uncover information that refutes the hypothesis, then change your hypothesis."

Source: Paul Saffo (technology forecaster, Stanford), attributed in: Ameet Ranadive, "Strong Opinions, Weakly Held — a framework for thinking," Medium, 2017-06-23. URL: https://medium.com/@ameet/strong-opinions-weakly-held-a-framework-for-thinking-6530d417e364 (Exa-retrieved). Also: Steve Blank, "Profound Beliefs," 2023-09-05. URL: https://steveblank.com/2023/09/05/profound-beliefs/ (Exa-retrieved).

*Note: The exact coinage "strong opinions, weakly held" is attributed to Paul Saffo in multiple sources, but a primary Saffo essay could not be directly retrieved. This attribution is widely accepted in the product community.*

### First Principles Thinking — Shane Parrish / Farnam Street

> "First principles thinking... is a tool to break down complicated problems by separating what we know is absolutely true from anything that is an assumption."

Applied to product analysis: strip away analogy-based reasoning (competitor X does Y, therefore we should too) and re-derive from verified user needs and business constraints.

Source: Shane Parrish, "What is First Principles Thinking?", fs.blog, 2018-04-09. URL: https://fs.blog/first-principles/ (Exa-retrieved). (Practitioner framing of an older philosophical concept)

## Encodable Thinking Moves

The following 13 directives are synthesized from the sources above. Each is formulated as a system-prompt-injectable instruction — specific enough to shape outputs, not so prescriptive that it prevents contextual judgment. Each is traced to a real source.

---

### TM-1: Derive Feature Value From the Underlying User Job

**Directive:** Before evaluating any feature, identify the user job it serves: state the situation, motivation, and expected outcome. Then trace the chain: job → current feature path → experience gap. Assign explanatory weight proportional to how much the feature closes the gap.

**Rationale:** Prevents analysis from floating at the level of features and UI, anchoring it to the functional reason users engage with the product.

**Source:** Jobs-to-Be-Done — Clayton Christensen (*The Innovator's Solution*, 2003); Tony Ulwick (*What Customers Want*, 2005). Operationalized in product analysis by Marty Cagan ("deep knowledge of users" means understanding *why* behavior occurs, not just *what* it is — SVPG, 2012).

---

### TM-2: Distinguish Metrics-Informed from Metrics-Driven Reasoning

**Directive:** Never treat a metric as a conclusion. Every quantitative finding must be paired with a qualitative interpretation: what behavior does this number represent? What does it not capture? State explicitly when data is being used as evidence vs. when qualitative judgment is filling the gap.

**Rationale:** Surface-level product commentary cites metrics as endpoints. Expert product thinking uses metrics as inputs to judgment, not substitutes for it.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, "Good PMs vs. Great PMs" thread (2020): "Great PMs make metrics-informed product decisions... masters of the art of blending quantitative and qualitative inputs."

---

### TM-3: De-risk on All Four Axes Before Recommending

**Directive:** Any product recommendation must address four risk dimensions: (1) Does the user actually want this? (value); (2) Can users figure out how to use it? (usability); (3) Can it be built? (feasibility); (4) Does it work for the business? (viability). A recommendation silent on any axis is incomplete.

**Rationale:** Generic commentary often only addresses one axis (usually value or feasibility). Expert analysis stress-tests all four — this is what makes recommendations defensible.

**Source:** Marty Cagan, *Inspired*, 2nd ed. (2017), "The Four Big Risks." Also SVPG, "The Product Manager Contribution" (2012).

---

### TM-4: State Every Claim's Epistemic Status

**Directive:** Tag each significant claim as one of: (a) empirically supported — cite the source and its nature; (b) expert opinion / practitioner judgment — attribute the source; (c) reasoned hypothesis — state the falsifiable version; (d) speculation — label it explicitly. Never leave epistemic status implicit.

**Rationale:** Generic analysis conflates hypothesis with fact. Expert analysis makes the basis of each claim visible so readers can calibrate weight.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, experiment discipline thread (2021): "When you are running an experiment, you need to specify your objectives, success/failure criteria, and decision tree upfront. Without these things, it becomes a Validation, not an Experiment." Also Paul Saffo / Strong Opinions Weakly Held: form a hypothesis, then actively seek disconfirmation.

---

### TM-5: Surface Trade-offs Explicitly — Name What Is Being Sacrificed

**Directive:** Every strategic choice must be accompanied by its trade-off: "Choosing X means explicitly deprioritizing Y for [time period]." Do not present a recommendation without naming the highest-value alternative it displaces.

**Rationale:** Surface commentary presents options as universally positive. Expert analysis recognizes that resources are finite and every choice is a rejection of alternatives. Opportunity cost thinking, not ROI thinking.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, Lenny Rachitsky podcast (2022): "ROI vs. opportunity cost thinking" — prioritize by minimizing foregone value, not by maximizing individual task returns. Also: Julie Zhuo, "How to Make Things High-Quality" (2019) — reframe quality/time tradeoffs prospectively.

---

### TM-6: Listen for What Isn't Said

**Directive:** When synthesizing user/customer evidence, explicitly note what users did not say, did not ask for, or showed through behavior rather than words. Surface the gap between stated preferences and inferred motivation.

**Rationale:** Novice analysis reports what users said. Expert analysis interprets the gap between request and need — like asking for a louder stereo when the real problem is engine noise.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, "Good/Great PMs" (2020): "Great PMs also listen to what isn't said." Ben Horowitz, "Good PM/Bad PM" (1998/2012): "probe deeper into the underlying problems... if you had a noisy car you might ask for a louder stereo, but you would probably be a lot happier with a quieter car."

---

### TM-7: Think at the Impact Level — Probe for Strategy Beneath Execution Problems

**Directive:** When observing a product execution failure (slow shipping, low quality, missed targets), do not accept execution as the root cause. Probe for the underlying strategy problem, incentive misalignment, or cultural issue. Label the level of analysis being applied: impact, execution, or optics.

**Rationale:** Most execution failures are strategy or structural problems in disguise. Expert analysis operates at the impact level; surface analysis stays at execution.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, Lenny Rachitsky podcast (2022): "Most Execution problems are really Strategy problems, Interpersonal problems, or Culture problems."

---

### TM-8: Imagine Failure Before It Happens (Pre-Mortem Move)

**Directive:** For any product strategy or recommendation, run a pre-mortem: assume the initiative has failed 12–18 months from now. Name the three most plausible causes. Distinguish real threats (Tigers) from perceived-but-unlikely threats (Paper Tigers) from unspoken concerns (Elephants). This surface of pre-mortem output should appear in any recommendation section.

**Rationale:** Analysis that only presents the optimistic case is not expert analysis. Expert analysts anticipate failure modes before they occur.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, Lenny Rachitsky podcast (2022): pre-mortem technique with Tigers/Paper Tigers/Elephants taxonomy.

---

### TM-9: Identify Leverage Points — Separate Multipliers from Adders

**Directive:** In any analysis of product features, strategic moves, or operational practices, distinguish leverage items (10x impact per unit of effort — removing them breaks everything) from neutral items (incremental gains) from overhead items (necessary but low return). Concentrate interpretive weight on leverage points.

**Rationale:** Generic product analysis treats all features as equally interesting. Expert analysis identifies the asymmetric bets — what is structural vs. incremental.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, LNO Framework (Twitter, Feb 2, 2020; formalized at coda.io/@shreyas/lno-framework): "Leverage tasks: where the effort has a 10x (or even 100x) return."

---

### TM-10: Apply the 5Qs Test to Any Product Claim

**Directive:** Any product recommendation or analysis claim should be evaluable against five questions: (1) Who is the customer? (2) What is the problem/opportunity? (3) What is the main customer benefit? (4) How do you know customers want this? (5) What is the customer experience? A claim that cannot answer all five is incomplete.

**Rationale:** These questions operationalize "product rigor" — they catch claims that skip from problem identification straight to solution advocacy without grounding.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, "PM Frameworks" thread (2021), "5Qs for product rigor." URL: https://threadreaderapp.com/thread/1399042778613485578.html

---

### TM-11: Ask "What Am I Getting Wrong?" — Build In Falsifiability

**Directive:** Every major conclusion must be accompanied by the strongest plausible counterargument. State the conditions under which this conclusion would be wrong. If you cannot name them, the conclusion is not sufficiently grounded.

**Rationale:** Weak analysis seeks validation. Expert analysis seeks falsification — it actively tries to break its own conclusions to test their strength.

**Source:** Shreyas Doshi, "Good/Great PMs" (2020): "Great PMs make it as easy as possible for colleagues to push back on their ideas & instincts. They often ask: 'What am I getting wrong?'" Also Paul Saffo's Strong Opinions Weakly Held: actively seek disconfirming evidence.

---

### TM-12: Distinguish What Users Do From What They Say (Quant + Qual Integration)

**Directive:** Never treat user interviews/surveys as ground truth without cross-referencing behavioral data. Explicitly reconcile discrepancies between stated preferences and observed behavior. When they conflict, name the conflict and reason through it.

**Rationale:** The most common form of superficial analysis trusts qualitative reports without checking behavioral reality, or trusts metrics without understanding underlying behavior.

**Source:** Marty Cagan: "The product manager needs to be the acknowledged expert on the customer. This means both quantitative learning (to understand what they are actually doing), and qualitative learning (to understand why our users and customers behave the way they do)" — SVPG, "The Product Manager Contribution" (2012). Also Shreyas Doshi: blending quantitative and qualitative inputs is the hallmark of great PMs.

---

### TM-13: Build the Product for Where the Market Will Be, Not Where It Was

**Directive:** When assessing a product's competitive position or strategic opportunities, situate the analysis at the forward trajectory of the market — where customer behavior, technology, and competitive dynamics are heading — not at their current or historical state. Any analysis anchored entirely in present conditions must be flagged as time-bounded.

**Rationale:** Expert product analysis is inherently prospective. Backward-looking analysis describes; forward-looking analysis advises.

**Source:** Marty Cagan, "The Product Manager Contribution" (2012): "We must create products for where the market will be tomorrow, not where it used to be yesterday." Also Julie Zhuo, product thinking definition: product thinkers "anticipate where the industry overall is headed when developing their product hypothesis."
